I was writing out financial applications in a DeFi company and we kept getting a lot of connection to our Postgres systems causing it to slow down the application. We decided to replicate the instance. This got me thinking how the distributed application coordination actually works. When I send a request to one of the instance and it's signifies done, how do I know that every other instance actually got that request? And that was the first time that I started to think about distributed systems. I was self-studying on this and came across the MIT course on distributed systems, and I decided to actually take it. While taking the course, we covered Raft. I picked up the Raft paper and read it and it felt very exciting (so much for academic paper). It also held up to it being understandable. I read the paper, and I got to implementing it. I implemented two components at first: Leader election and Log replication, but then lost that implementation when I changed my laptop (didn't push most of my changes). Determined to fully understand distributed consensus from more than an academic lens, I decided to re-read the paper (after some months), and reimplement it all over again.

Without a system like Raft, we cannot trust that our data is replicated across our various replicas. Distributed nodes faces multiple problems from crashes to partitions to stale nodes. Raft is an algorithm that allows all nodes come to agreement on the current state making sure that our system stays consistent even in the midst of these problems. The core idea behind raft is that the leader dictates what the state is; Only a node with an up to date log can become leader and an entry is considered committed after a majority of nodes have it. That majority requirement is everything - as long as more than half the cluster is alive and connected, the system makes progress and never loses committed data

Election is used to choose which of the nodes becomes the source of truth for all remaining replicas. Leader election in Raft is guided by a constraint which is that only a node with the most up to date log can become leader. This is important because in Raft, nodes follow the leaders log and can overwrite their logs to match the leaders so we want to make sure that a stale node isn't elected leader causing committed entries to get overwritten which is the one golden rule for consistency in our system. Implementing leader election is pretty straightforward. Digesting the material made it a little effortless. There are a couple of small things to note but something to surface from my journey is resetting the votedFor for a node when it receives a request for vote from a node on a higher term than it is. I had a subtle intermittent fault which causes no leader to be elected in an election round and could span multiple rounds. This means you loose availability in that moment and the cluster cannot process client request.

Log replication is the bulk of the system. Once a leader is elected, the leader needs to replicate its log across the cluster for consistency. Implementing log replication required care because there are a lot of constraint from leader election that also applies. For example, if an elected leader gets a response from a follower with a term higher than its (this is during log replication), the leader must step down. This subtlety is required for the correctness of the system but also introduces a bug that can impact the health of your cluster, more on that in the bug section. During my implementation, one thing of note that seemed like I glanced through in the paper but raised a problem in the system is that the Leader can only commit entries in its current term. The leader is responsible for setting the highest commit index of the log, and the leader sets the commit index to the index of an entry once that entry is replicated on a majority of the server - and only if that entry was created in its current term. Why the leader's term ? this makes sure that the leader's log is replicated on a majority of the servers and prevents a node with a more up to date log from becoming leader in the future and overwriting committed entries. It's fun to think about this failure scenario that's subtle and to think that a simple line of code missing impacts all the correctness of your protocol. A more indepth discussion about the issue is here https://dev.to/abdellani/why-raft-cant-safely-commit-old-term-entries-370p

Another implementation bug I ran into was a race issue that happened while updating the nextIndex for a follower when I have multiple concurrent processes (say 3 for example) sending append entries to followers. Care needs to be taken on the way to update the nextIndex. Originally I  had nextIndex += len(entries) so nextIndex ends up at 1 + 3*len(entries) instead of 1 + len(entries). The leader then thinks the follower is further ahead than it is. The fix was to set nextIndex = max(nextIndex, prevLogIndex + len(entries) + 1) instead of just incrementing it.

Snaphotting was interesting. It's hard to see the importance when you're running the nodes for a few seconds in test and with a controlled client but it becomes ever more important when deployed in production. I found that getting snapshotting right required being careful about the log indexing. Let me explain. When you send entries over to a follower, you send the previous log index and term just before the first entry you sent. The follower simply uses that index to get its previous log entry and do the required constraint check. All seems well. Now, snapshotting changes the index of the entries because it discards entries up to a point and remembers only the last included index. When you snapshot indexes 1 to 5 (of a 8-length log slice) and store the result (the snapshot) in index 0 of your log, your log index might have gotten smaller (log length is now 4) but your raft index still stays the same. This must be because there could exists a follower that is not up to date and their log length is 5 entries longer than the leader's slice (although their raft index matches) because they do not have the snapshot the leader has yet. If a leader naively uses its log index as reference for the previous log index during append entries RPCs, log replication would fail. So, I had to review every part of the code that uses the log index and differentiate between the log index (the index of entries in the slice) and raft index (the index of entries in the raft entries as applied by the client) and adjusted the raft index to match the snapshotting. The way to do this is to remember that raftIndex = logIndex + lastIncludedIndex.

When the TC-S2 test kept failing, I very much thought it was something with the code at first. I had struggled with the off by one error of the raft index and log index and I just thought I had another hidden bug in the code. Further analysis showed me that this was beyond the code. The bug was intermittent so it was really hard to track. It required debug logs to finally put a label to the fault. The test was to confirm that a stale node would get a snapshot entry from the leader given the node was stale long enough for the leader to create a snapshot. To model this, I created a laggard node. It got partitioned once the network came up. I sent some requests to the leader and then a snapshot of some of the committed and applied entries. Then I reconnected this partitioned laggard node expecting that to catch it up the leader would send it the snapshot it had and the remaining entries not included in the snapshot. To my surprise, the laggard ended up with all the log entries instead without the snapshot. Odd ? indeed. How did the laggard end up with the log entries that have been discarded ? This lead me to believe the problem was not the code but the test harness itself (all other test were passing) and the culprit after adding debug logs was the Network simulator. To explain, each append request is a separate goroutine, this means we do not control when they are scheduled by the go scheduler. The scenario causing the bug happened because the goroutine to send the append entries to the crashed laggard had not yet been scheduled by the time the client created a snapshot, and the laggard got restarted. It seemed like all that could go wrong did go wrong. Then once the laggard came up, the goroutine got scheduled and delivers the append entries request to the laggard containing the whole log of the leader prior to the snapshot. By the time the append entry from the leader for the logs with the snapshot comes, the laggard is up to date, hence it never receives the snapshot and the test just waits to receive the snapshot, doesn't get it and then fails. The fix to this is to sleep on the test after the snapshot is taken and before the laggard is restarted to allow go routines that built up stale request to drain. Any goroutine that built a stale request either runs and fails while the laggard is still down, or never gets a chance to run against a live laggard. The original instinct was to change the protocol itself. Tracking the lastIncludedIndex at the moment a request was sent, and discarded the response if the snapshot had advanced by the time it came back. That reduced failures but did not eliminate them. More importantly it was the wrong layer — the protocol was correct, bending it to pass a test was hiding the real problem. When writing distributed applications, care needs to be taken on the various layers of your system. The fault was with the test and not the protocol itself.

The core algorithm of the protocol, the leader election, log replication, log compaction I wrote by hand. Those require understanding the protocol itself. I used claude code to write test cases for every components after writing them. Claude also helped in multiple debugging sessions when I get stuck and scaffolded the UI for visualizing the protocol (you can interact with that on the repo). I finally had the Claude check the code for Go idioms and whether the implementation matched the paper but the tests were the deciding factor. If the tests passed the implementation was correct, not because the AI said so. I mention this because the honest version is more useful than the sanitised one — the algorithm is the hard part, the tooling is just tooling.

A node running without observability is a black box. To see what was happening when something goes wrong (something eventually does), I had to add in telemetry. This helps me observe the entire health of my cluster. I collected majorly metrics and that allowed me to see how many elections were taking place (which is a good indicator of the protocol health), how many nodes were lagging behind the leader, how many nodes were not participating in the network and the overall traffic in the network. Something else that became visible which I had encountered in test was the term explosion that happens when a node is partitioned. One interesting thing about setting up telemetry is that you only need one instrumentation layer (OTEL) and you can swap backend without touching the code.

There's a good feeling you get when your system is actually running and not just passing tests. To see my system run means that I need to put them on a real network. Placing the system on a real network required a little extra work but not as much as it could have been if I had not designed that from the start with the Peer interface representing a raft peer. The interface takes a Call method to send a request to a peer. The Call method could be anything, a gRPC, a test harness, the engine never knew the difference. Finally the UI allows me to visualize the system in a way that you cannot just do with the terminal. You can watch elections happen in real time, see the leader step down, partition a node and watch the cluster recover, watch a node get caught up to the leader's log and much more.

No implementation from scratch comes without surprises and mine was no different. While reading the raft paper, I missed a tiny detail - a leader only commits entries in it's own term. This sounded trivial if you glanced over the doc at first and it's also just a line of code but as you would see from the test that it can make or break your protocol. Fully read and digest the paper when implementing. Another fascinating surprise was that a partitioned node inflated its term and disrupted the cluster on rejoining. This seemed at first to me like I missed something from the paper and I had to check but saw that I didn't miss anything. This being a feature of the protocol is odd. Some implementations, like etcd, address this with a pre-vote mechanism. Nonetheless it was odd to see that this wasn't discussed in the papers. One place I spent a surprising time on was the raft index vs the slice index I had earlier discussed. First it was easy to miss somewhere in the protocol where I need to change to using raft index and also I had a lot of off-by-one errors when making those adjustments. This is the result of progressive implementation - optimizations might require comprehensive refactor and those always know how to surprise you.

Implementing from a paper can seem daunting sometimes but it definitely gives you something just reading the paper does not and that is the practical knowledge. The Raft paper made the protocol understandable and also enlightened me on how to approach implementing papers. Break the components down, implement one component after the other, and test each component as you implement. The first step is picking the paper up and just reading it. Then take a component of the paper (the basic component to reduce dependency) and implement that, test it, and that gives you the drive to carry on.

I've wondered how distributed systems come to agreement on the state of a system. This came up again when I was building out a DeFi applications and we had multiple instances of distributed database, and I just wondered how we can be sure that records are replicated across all these instances. So I decided to implement a consensus protocol. Surprisingly, I found it not as daunting as I thought at first. Many of the bugs that I encountered were because of some oversight on my part in reading the paper thoroughly and also in my test harness, which seems odd that a test harness could actually cause a consensus protocol failure.
